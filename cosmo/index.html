Background

With support from PCO’s Public Opinion Research (POR) Unit and endorsement from communications and policy partners across the Government of Canada – in particular within the health portfolio – Impact Canada launched the COVID-19 Snapshot Monitoring (COSMO) study in Canada in early April 2020.

+ Learn more

More background

Under a longitudinal design, data from a cohort of approximately 2,000 Canadians are collected through an online survey every two to four weeks in eight waves of data collection. Given the valuable insights and intelligence COSMO has provided, and its immediate integration into the COVID policy and communications response, the survey has been extended to the end of March 2021, with up to eight additional waves of data collection.

Compared to traditional public opinion polling, the longitudinal design of the COSMO survey enables the gathering of information that can lead to deep insights about how the views and behaviours of citizens are changing over time as the COVID-19 crisis evolves.

This rich data can support immediate policy objectives, such as more precise targeting of government messaging and communications to priority areas to encourage particular behaviours. Given the depth and breadth of the study, it can also provide signals about longer-term implications that might require new or adapted policy responses.

Study objectives

Overall, research questions relate to:

Risk perceptions

How changes in risk perceptions may relate to knowledge and misinformation;

Participant Awareness

Whether participants report that they are aware of specific response measures and whether being aware of them influences risk perceptions;

Preparedness and behaviours

Whether risk perceptions are related to preparedness and the use of protective behaviours;

Knowledge vs. misinformation

Knowledge and misinformation about preparedness and preventive measures and whether the level of knowledge is related to certain sources of information; and

Government help reactions

Reactions to announcements and programs from the Government of Canada to help combat the COVID-19 outbreak, and its associated effects.;

Methodology

Organization (collectively titled the WHO Behavioural Insights (BI) Tool) to support nations in tracking the various ways citizens are being affected by COVID-19. The resources – including a comprehensive survey, guidance on its implementation, and the files required to analyze the data and create visuals – allows for rapid and robust data collection.

More about the methodology

The participants for this study were recruited through an online panel managed by Leger Consulting, a Canadian research firm. The results are weighted using 2016 Statistics Canada Census data to mirror the population distribution in Canada. The sampling frame consists of 50% women for each age group (18-34; 35-54; 55+) and region across the country. Data also captures employment status pre- and post-COVID-19, level of education, size of community, status as health care worker or front-line worker, years living in Canada, household income, number of people living in the home, sexual orientation, chronic disease status, ethnicity, and place of birth. There is no associated margin of error for the results, since a non-probability design means that results cannot be projected to the larger population.

Data Collection during an Evolving Pandemic

When interpreting the results of the COSMO study, attention should be made to the state of the pandemic in Canada at the time of each data collection wave. At the start of the study, in April 2020, case numbers were peaking with most Canadians believing we were experiencing the worst of the crisis or that the worst was yet to come.

  • More information

    Data collection waves 3 to 5, on the other hand, occurred in a time of declining case numbers, with more and more Canadians believing the worst of the crisis was behind us. As Canada experienced a resurgence in case numbers in late August and into September, public risk perception also increased (data collected in waves 6 to 8).

Study population

Data was collected through an online survey with adult Canadians aged 18 years and older, randomly recruited through an online panel managed by Leger Consulting, a Canadian research firm. The data has been weighted to ensure that the sample distribution reflects the actual Canadian adult population according to Statistics Canada census data. There is no associated margin of error for the results, since a non-probability design means that results cannot be projected to the larger population.

Results

A number of selected figures are presented below. For more information on the data set, please contact the Impact and Innovation Unit.

1

Public Risk Perceptions

More and more Canadians are feeling that the worst of the crisis is yet to come. Up until Wave 5 of the survey, there was a steady increase in the number of respondents who thought “the worst of the crisis is behind us,” however, from Wave 5 to Wave 8 the percentage has declined from 41% to 26%. The percentage of respondents who think that the “worst of the crisis is yet to come” increased from 35% in Wave 5 to 49% by Wave 8. Certain groups are more likely to think that the worst is yet to come, including Canadians aged 55+ (57%) and residents of BC + YK (64%).

2

Information Sources

Trust in government sources for information on COVID-19 have gradually declined from Wave 1 but have remained steady over the last four Waves.

+ View data set
3

Compliance with Protective Behaviours

There has consistently been high use of frequent hand washing, and use of hand sanitizer across all waves. There were significant decreases over the first few waves especially with physical distancing, staying home when sick, and self-quarantine. Compliance has remained steady over the last few waves. Across waves, there has been a significant increase in wearing a mask when out in public.

+ View data set
4

Privacy

There is still a lack of consensus in Canadians’ perceptions about the federal government using cell phone data to monitor the movement of individuals who are supposed to be self-isolating.

+ View data set
5

Vaccine confidence

While almost two thirds of Canadians agree that they would get a recommended vaccine, there is variation across age and gender groups. Older respondents (males and females) are much more likely to agree that they would get a recommended vaccine, relative to all other age groups.

+ View data set
6

Returning to "Normal"

Perceptions of acceptable timelines to relax public health measures vary considerably. Canadians report that restrictions on outdoor spaces and non-essential services should be lifted now or soon. Canadians report that travel restrictions, physical distancing and the cancelling of large gatherings should be extended at least 6 months more.

7

Travel Intentions and Border Measures

The number of respondents that disagree that international travel (non US) should resume (~half) has remained steady over the last 3 waves. The same is true for the number of respondents that disagree that travel restrictions between Canada and the US (majority).

Next steps

The Impact Canada Behavioural Science team will use a series of statistical analyses to look for patterns in the data, allowing us to answer questions like:

  • How do intentions to vaccinate change over time?
  • How do changes in trust in government information sources affect changes in intentions to vaccinate over time?
  • How do individual-level characteristics (e.g., gender, baseline knowledge or anxiety, infection status) affect changes in intentions to vaccinate over time?
  • How do interactions between gender and trust in government information sources affect changes in intentions to vaccinate over time?

We’re also preparing to advance a second phase of data collection, extending the project into 2021!
